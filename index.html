What does this analysis tell us?

This analysis summarises some of the published data on Coronavirus (Covid-19). It shows changes in confirmed cases over time as well as the mortality related to Covid-19.

This is primarily for the Sussex Local Authorities (Brighton and Hove, East Sussex, and West Sussex), but it also covers the 19 UTLAs in the South East region of England.

There are lots of stories to tell with the data, but it should be noted that our understanding is evolving daily as new information is received and as the data is revised. The box below shows a few important things to remember whilst exploring this analyses.

  • What we know about the spread of Covid-19 is a reflection of testing suspected cases.
  • Data on the death toll of Covid-19 comes from several different sources, each offering different perspectives as well as having different caveats. These should be considered carefully.
  • There are frequent changes in who are counted in statistics as well as changes in populations being tested for Covid-19 as capacity increases over time. Spikes in confirmed cases and deaths can be the result of virus spread as well as the way information is recorded.
  • Over time, new data will be added that will be more accurate and offer deeper understanding of the impact of Covid-19 on health and wellbeing.

Confirmed cases

Data are back dated and revised such that every lab-confirmed case is attributed to the date at which the specimin was taken, which means the outbreak starts on different dates for different areas. The first specimins for a confirmed Covid-19 infection were taken on Janurary 30th 2020.

The first table and visualisation shows the most recently recorded confirmed cases and the number of new confirmed cases by day. It is ordered by total number (cumulative) of confirmed cases to date with the highest number of confirmed cases at the top. You can toggle between viewing the coloured tiles as actual confirmed cases or as confirmed cases per 100,000 population (crude, all age population as at 2018).

The cumulative cases are taken from the most recently available date, although number of confirmed cases in a single day (a proxy for new cases) is taken from six days prior (latest complete date).

The areas across the South East have vast differences in population sizes.

The smallest area in terms of population, Bracknell Forest, is estimated to be home for around 121,600 residents whilst Kent, with the biggest population, is estimated to be home for almost 13 times Bracknell Forest's population with around 1,568,600 people estimated to live there.

Given the differences in population, you might naturally expect there to be more cases in one area than another. Viewing the cases per 100,000 allows you to compare across areas as if they had the same population size.

The colour key for the tiles has been chosen to capture the breadth of new cases across the region, whilst being sensitive to small numbers as well as periods of no reported cases.

The colour key may change as the number of cases are expected to increase in coming weeks.

It is important to consider the testing strategy, capacity limitations, and any lag or delay in testing results, as outlined above, when interpreting cases as this can have an impact on the daily figures for local areas.

A Surge in cases can in part be due to the amount of testing done locally from one day to the next.

A longer doubling time indicates slower growth and a shorter doubling time indicates growth rate is increasing.

A note on testing and data

  • The current (April) strategy has largely been to prioritise testing among those patients with suspected Covid-19 symptoms in intensive care units within hospitals, then in isolated cluster outbreak settings such as care homes as well as a small sample of general practices carrying out random testing for surveillance purposes (BMJ, 2020).
  • As capacity increases, more and more healthcare workers as well as other keyworkers are being prioritised for testing in what has been termed 'Pillar 2' testing, which includes testing from commercial partners. From 29th April 2020, anyone aged over 65 as well as anyone who must leave their home to go to work will be able to have a swab test.
  • Beyond this, the government is planning to expand diagnostics to antibody testing to confirm that a person has had the virus and is therefore likely to be immune from catching it again. See Government testing plans April 6th 2020 for more details.

  • From the 14th April, PHE changed the method of reporting to show confirmed cases by the date at which the specimin for testing was taken. The first positive specimen date is used and duplicate tests for the same patient are removed. Whilst this method more accurately tracks the dates at which people are known to be infected, it may look like cases on the most recent days (which are still being reported daily) are sloping off (getting smaller). This is not the case, it is simply that the most recent testing results may take several days to be reported. PHE suggest that data for the most recent five days should be treated with caution and considered 'incomplete'.

Cumulative cases over time from a different perspective

So far, we have compared areas over time using counts and rates. These have shown vast differences across areas, and particularly highlighted that some local areas might be much further along their individual outbreaks than others. Indeed, we see that some areas such as Kent are recording their 2,000th confirmed case whilst the 50th case is being reported on the Isle of Wight.

As some areas record their first few confirmed cases on different days, we have redrawn the x axis (along the bottom from left to right) on the next figure to count the number of days since case number 10. By doing this, you pull all of the trajectories to start at the same point on the figure and they can be compared with other areas on this basis. Starting on case number 10, rather than case number 1, means that the trajectories are a little more established and potentially showing transmission within an area as opposed to single cases coming into the area.

In addition, we have redrawn the y (vertical) axis to show the cumulative number of confirmed cases on a logarithmic scale (briefly explained on the right) to particularly highlight changes in growth (speeding up or slowing down) of infections. Other measures of Covid-19 spread

The data presented so far has been based on confirmed cases of Covid-19. The confirmed cases are only a representation of the testing strategy and currently only show test results for patients in hospital as well as 'frontline' healthcare workers. There will be many undiagnosed cases in the community (outside of hospital), in carehomes, and in other settings (such as prisons).

Until a robust community testing capability is realised, it is impossible to define the true spread of Covid-19. However, there are some other data sources and measures that can help to fill in the gaps.

Symptom tracker applications such as the 'COVID Symptom Tracker' by researchers at King's College London, Guys and St Thomas' Hospitals, and ZOE Global ltd. This allows users to enter how they are physically feeling daily along with information on their sex, age, ethnicity, and first part of their postcode. Users can say if they are feeling well and have no symptoms or to indicate that they are symptomatic. Whilst much of this detailed data is not publicly available, it is possible to see at lower tier local authority level, the proportion of users who report at least some symptoms of the Covid-19 virus. In the UK (as at 18th April), there were 462,672 people aged 20-69 who reported currently having symptoms of the virus. Of course, not all those with symptoms will use the application, and the local figures do not include those under the age of 20 or over the age of 69.

Another source of information comes from the the NHS 111 symptom reporting pathways and 111 online. This shows, at the level of Clinical Commissioning Groups (which are largely coterminous with local authority boundaries through aggregation), the number of pathway triages and online assessments in which a user reports Covid-19 symptoms. Data include assessments by sex and broad age group (0-18, 19-69, and 70-120 years) of the user. There are many caveats around this, including that the data are not counts of people but rather assessments, people with symptoms might not use this or might use it multiple times over the course of their symptoms (and maybe not at the start of their symptoms). People are encouraged NOT to call NHS 111 to report Covid-19 symptoms unless they are worried but are asked to complete an NHS 111 online triage assessment. However, this data may give some indication of the symptom reporting locally.

Both the COVID Symptom Tracker, and NHS triage data, are for reporting of symptoms and they are not in anyway representative of confirmed cases of Covid-19.

Mortality

This section focuses on the burden of Covid-19 in terms of the number of lives lost as a result of the virus.

When a person dies, their death is certified and registered formally with the registry office for the local authority district in which the person lived. If a person dies in hospital deaths, their death is registered with the district office in which the hospital is located. A death should be registered within five days of the death occurring, and upon the issue of a medical certificate detailing the cause or causes of death although registration can be delayed if the death has been reported to the coroner and an investigation or inquest is required.

These registration processes naturally take time and so NHS England, and now the Care Quality Commission, have begun reporting the number of deaths they have been notified as soon as it is practicably possible to support the response to Covid-19.

There are at least three sources of data for understanding the number of Covid-19 deaths. A summary of the key differences in the three sources is given in the table below.

A note on mortality data sources
Department for Health and Social Care Office for National Statistics Care Quality Commission
What is counted? Deaths in hospitals, care homes, and in the wider community where the deceased had a positive Covid-19 test result confirmed by a Public Health or NHS laboratory. Daily figures are based on the date of reporting and not on the date of occurrence. All deaths registered in England and Wales, by place of death (Hospital, Care home, Hospice, Home) by the week in which the death occurred. Care home deaths where the care home provider has stated COVID-19 as a suspected or confirmed cause of death on the death notification.
Geographical granularity Hospital Trust level Lower Tier Local Authority Lower Tier Local Authority
Deaths are included where patient has been tested for COVID-19. Since 1st April 2020, deaths are included where COVID-19 has been mentioned anywhere on the death certificate.
How often are data released Data are provided daily by NHS Trusts and PHE Health protection teams to NHS England and only once confirmed family have been notified of the death. Data are provided weekly, based on the date on which the death occurred with a lag of just over one week to allow for registering. Death notifications by date of notification are provided weekly at the same time as the ONS release
Are the data complete? Whilst data can be provided rapidly, it is not always possible and data providers suggest that data in the most recent five days should be considered incomplete. The data do not include deaths of people who had Covid-19 but had not been tested, people who were tested positive only via a non-NHS or Public Health laboratory, or people who subsequently caught the virus and died following a negative test result. Estimates prior to 2020 suggest around 26% of deaths in England and Wales are registered later than seven calendar days after occurance, with the proportion as high as 32% in the South East region. Deaths occuring in the last week but not registered within 7 days are not included in the latest weeks figures but may be added in future releases. Death notifications take on average 4 days to receive and process. These are not officially registered deaths and can be subject to revision and verification. Data are for April 10th onwards.
Adapted from the Office for National Statistics and Public Health England

It is very difficult to know the true impact of Covid-19 quickly, it may take many weeks and months to understand which deaths can be attributed to the virus directly or indirectly. These figures help to build as complete a picture as possible.

Overall number of deaths attributed to Covid-19

The national figures covering all settings are reported daily. However, to understand mortality at a local level using publicly available data we have to look at the individual settings and for deaths occuring outside of hospital, there is some time delay in reporting. There are more timely figures for hospital deaths, which are explored further down in the analysis, but these are reported at Acute Hospital Trust and this is not always coterminous with residential populations of local authorities.

The ONS has begun publishing weekly data at lower tier local authority for the number of deaths occurring in each week in the 2020 calendar year. From 31 March 2020 these figures also show the number of deaths involving coronavirus (COVID-19), based on any mention of COVID-19 on the death certificate. These are aggregated to upper tier local authority for this analysis.
